CPL CHECK RIDE PREPARATION CHECKLIST

Your Complete Guide to Passing Your Commercial Pilot Check Ride

SECTION 1: PHYSICAL PREPARATION (3 Days Before)

☐ Sleep at least 7 hours (non-negotiable)

Why it matters: A fatigued brain flying a commercial maneuver is completely different from a rested one. This is not negotiable.
  • Go to bed early (3 nights before)
  • Avoid screens 1 hour before bed
  • Aim for 7-8 hours minimum
💡 I slept 8 hours the night before my check ride. Best decision ever.

☐ Eat a proper meal

Why it matters: Your brain and body need fuel. Hungry pilots fly poorly.
  • Eat balanced meals (protein, carbs, veggies)
  • Avoid heavy foods that make you sluggish
  • Eat on time (don't skip meals)
💡 I had oatmeal with fruit and coffee 2 hours before my check ride. Perfect fuel.

☐ Hydrate well throughout the day

Why it matters: Dehydration causes brain fog, anxiety, and poor decision-making.
  • Drink water consistently (not all at once)
  • Avoid excessive energy drinks
  • Bring water bottle to airport
💡 I drank ~2 liters of water the day before. Felt sharp.

☐ Avoid excessive caffeine

Why it matters: Too much caffeine = jitters, anxiety, poor focus. Bad combo during check ride.
  • Limit to 1-2 cups of coffee/tea
  • Avoid energy drinks the day before
  • No caffeine after lunch
💡 Normal coffee is fine. Just don't overdo it.

☐ Do light exercise (walking, stretching)

Why it matters: Exercise reduces anxiety and clears your head. Light movement is key (not intense).
  • 20-30 minute walk
  • Stretching routine
  • Light yoga
  • Nothing strenuous
💡 I took a 30-minute walk the afternoon before. Cleared my head completely.

☐ Arrive at airport 45 minutes early

Why it matters: Rushing = stress. Early arrival = calm, collected mindset.
  • Plan your route to airport
  • Leave early to avoid traffic
  • Arrive with time to spare
  • Do NOT rush through preflight
💡 I arrived 45 minutes early. Preflighted slowly and deliberately. No stress.

☐ Preflight slowly and deliberately

Why it matters: Rushing preflight causes you to miss things. Slow, methodical preflight shows the examiner your standard.
  • Walk around the aircraft slowly
  • Use your preflight checklist from the POH
  • Check every item carefully
  • Narrate what you're checking
💡 My examiner watched my preflight. Slow, deliberate checking impressed him.

SECTION 2: MENTAL PREPARATION (2 Days Before)

☐ Review ACS tolerances for every required task

Why it matters: The Airman Certification Standards (ACS) is your ENTIRE roadmap. Know tolerances cold.
  • Print your ACS document
  • Highlight all tolerance standards
  • Memorize: Altitude ±100 ft, Airspeed ±10 kts, Heading ±10°, Bank ±5°
  • Tape tolerances to your kneeboard
💡 I taped tolerances to my kneeboard. Examiner was impressed with my knowledge.

☐ Study systems and procedures thoroughly

Why it matters: The oral exam will ask you to EXPLAIN systems, not just define them.
  • Study your aircraft's systems deeply
  • Understand WHY things work the way they do
  • Be able to explain to a non-pilot
  • Practice explaining out loud
💡 Examiner asked me to walk through fuel imbalance scenarios. I had to THINK, not memorize.

☐ Brief emergency procedures

Why it matters: You need to know what to do if things go wrong. This builds confidence.
  • Read emergency section of POH
  • Know engine failure procedures
  • Know systems failures procedures
  • Practice saying them out loud
�� I briefed emergency procedures calmly. It reduced my anxiety significantly.

☐ Visualize maneuvers

Why it matters: Mental practice is as important as flying. Visualization reduces anxiety and improves performance.
  • Close your eyes and imagine flying perfectly
  • Feel the airplane moving
  • Imagine successful completion
  • Do this 5-10 minutes per day
💡 I visualized a perfect chandelle every night. By check ride, it felt automatic.

☐ Review cross-country flight plan

Why it matters: You'll be given a cross-country scenario. Being familiar helps.
  • Get the likely test area from your DPE
  • Plan a realistic cross-country route
  • Mark waypoints on sectional
  • Know checkpoints and estimated times
💡 I planned my route the day before and briefed it 3 times. Nailed the briefing on check ride.

☐ Practice explaining systems out loud

Why it matters: Talking about systems while nervous is hard. Practice makes it smooth.
  • Explain aircraft systems to your instructor
  • Explain to a friend who doesn't fly
  • Record yourself explaining
  • Do this for every major system
💡 My instructor and I spent 2 hours just talking. This practice was GOLD on the oral.

☐ Do a final mock oral with your instructor

Why it matters: This is your dress rehearsal. It shows you what to expect.
  • Schedule 2-hour mock oral session
  • Have your instructor ask random questions
  • Don't let them go easy
  • Get feedback on what to improve
💡 My mock oral was tough. But it made the real thing feel easy.

SECTION 3: AIRCRAFT PREPARATION (2 Days Before)

☐ Confirm AROW documents are present and current

Why it matters: Without these, you cannot fly legally. The examiner will check.
  • Airworthiness Certificate (current)
  • Registration Certificate (current)
  • Operating Limitations (POH or AFM)
  • Weight & Balance (current)
💡 I confirmed AROW 2 days before and 1 day before. No surprises.

☐ Verify weight & balance calculations are complete

Why it matters: Wrong weight & balance = unsafe flight. Examiner will verify your calculation.
  • Get latest aircraft weight & balance data
  • Weigh yourself (pilot + clothing)
  • Calculate fuel load and total weight & CG
  • Ensure within limits and bring calculation
💡 I calculated W&B the night before and rechecked morning of. No issues.

☐ Review performance charts for your aircraft

Why it matters: You need to know takeoff distance, landing distance, climb performance, etc.
  • Get current weather forecast
  • Use performance charts for those conditions
  • Calculate required runway length
  • Know altitude performance and bring charts
💡 My examiner asked "Can we depart?" I had charts ready and was confident.

☐ Check NOTAMs and TFRs for test area

Why it matters: Temporary flight restrictions or NOTAMs could affect your check ride.
  • Check Federal NOTAM Search
  • Check for TFRs
  • Check for airspace closures
  • Check for equipment outages at airports
💡 I checked NOTAMs 2 days before. Nothing unusual. Felt prepared.

☐ Review weather forecast for check ride day

Why it matters: You need to know what conditions to expect.
  • Check 7-day forecast
  • Know wind conditions and ceiling
  • Know temperature/dew point
  • Know any advisories
💡 Weather was perfect on my check ride day. Still reviewed thoroughly.

☐ Confirm fuel requirements are met

Why it matters: Insufficient fuel = check ride failure or worse.
  • Calculate fuel needed for full test (3-4 hours)
  • Add 30-45 minutes reserve
  • Request full fuel load at least night before
  • Confirm fuel truck availability
💡 I requested full fuel the night before. No issues day-of.

☐ Test all avionics equipment thoroughly

Why it matters: Non-functioning avionics = problems during flight test.
  • Test radio (both comm and nav)
  • Test autopilot and GPS/moving map
  • Test backup instruments and lights
  • Test each system twice
💡 I tested all avionics the day before. Found one radio acting weird and fixed it.

SECTION 4: DAY-OF PREPARATION (Check Ride Day)

☐ Pack all required documents (cert, medical, ID)

Why it matters: You cannot do a check ride without legal documents.
  • ✓ Pilot Certificate (current)
  • ✓ Medical Certificate (current)
  • ✓ Government-issued Photo ID
  • ✓ Logbook and Endorsements
💡 I packed everything night before and checked again morning-of. Zero stress.

☐ Review map and routing one more time

Why it matters: Fresh review clears your mind and builds confidence.
  • Pull out your sectional chart
  • Review your planned route
  • Brief the route one more time (out loud)
  • Review checkpoints and altitudes
💡 Morning of, I reviewed my route in the parking lot. Built confidence.

☐ Do thorough preflight with focus

Why it matters: This sets the tone for your entire check ride.
  • Use your aircraft's preflight checklist
  • Move SLOWLY and DELIBERATELY
  • Check every single item
  • Narrate what you're checking
💡 My examiner watched my entire preflight. Slow, thorough checking impressed him.

☐ Brief yourself before takeoff

Why it matters: This is your final mental preparation before flying.
  • Sit in cockpit and close eyes for 30 seconds
  • Take deep breath
  • Brief route, altitude, cruise speed
  • Visualize success
💡 I did a 5-minute self brief in the cockpit. Mental reset was powerful.

☐ Do a final radio check

Why it matters: Makes sure your radio is working before departing.
  • Tune to ATIS and listen
  • Call ground with brief radio check
  • Verify they hear you clearly
  • Confirm aircraft call sign
💡 Everything was clear. Felt confident in radio work.

SECTION 5: DURING CHECK RIDE (In The Air)

☐ Brief yourself before each maneuver

Why it matters: Deliberate briefing = better execution and confidence.
  • State altitude and airspeed
  • State configuration and recovery criteria
  • Pause and focus
  • Execute maneuver (takes 30 seconds per maneuver)
💡 My instructor taught me this. By check ride, examiner saw me thinking deliberately.

☐ Use proper phraseology naturally (don't sound robotic)

Why it matters: You're a pilot, not a robot. Natural, professional communication is key.
  • Use proper phraseology from AIM
  • Don't sound like you memorized it
  • Sound professional but natural
  • Speak clearly and concisely
💡 I practiced radio work casually with instructor. By check ride, it felt natural.

☐ Treat examiner like a first officer (not a judge)

Why it matters: This mindset shift is MASSIVE. You're a professional crew.
  • Narrate your decisions
  • Call out traffic and explain reasoning
  • Brief them on what you're doing
  • Demonstrate command authority
💡 Instead of being nervous, I thought "I'm flying with a crew member." Game changer.

☐ Manage aircraft and workload simultaneously

Why it matters: Commercial pilots manage multiple tasks at once.
  • Fly the plane first
  • Then manage radios and navigation
  • Keep eyes outside
  • Adjust for wind/weather
💡 Prioritize flying the plane. Everything else follows.

☐ Maintain situational awareness at all times

Why it matters: You need to know where you are and where traffic is.
  • Scan outside (primary)
  • Quick glances at instruments
  • Monitor radio for traffic
  • Always know nearest airport
💡 I was always aware of nearest airport. Safety first.

☐ Ask for clarification if unsure about anything

Why it matters: Guessing = failure. Asking = professionalism.
  • If examiner asks unclear question: "Can you clarify that?"
  • If uncertain about instruction, confirm it
  • If need to look something up, ask permission
  • Professional pilots always verify
💡 Examiner asked regulation I wasn't sure of. He smiled when I looked it up professionally.

SECTION 6: LANDING & FINISHING (End of Check Ride)

☐ Execute soft-field landing with precision

Why it matters: Soft-field landings separate commercial pilots from private pilots.
  • Maintain full back pressure during flare
  • Touch down as gently as possible
  • Keep nose up and use minimal braking
  • Demonstrate aircraft control
💡 I practiced soft-field landings every flight in my last month. It paid off.

☐ Execute short-field landing with precision

Why it matters: Another commercial-exclusive maneuver. Show mastery.
  • Steep descent angle and accurate touchpoint
  • Aggressive braking if needed
  • Show forward slip capability
  • Land in designated touchdown zone
💡 Short-field landings were my weak point. Practiced 20+ times and finally nailed it.

☐ Complete post-landing procedures smoothly

Why it matters: Professional finish to your check ride.
  • Slow aircraft smoothly
  • Use proper flap retraction
  • Follow ATC instructions
  • Taxi safely and complete shutdown
💡 Even at the end, I stayed methodical. Professional finish matters.

☐ Debrief thoroughly with examiner

Why it matters: This is when you learn if you passed and get feedback.
  • Listen to examiner feedback
  • Ask questions if unclear
  • Thank them for the check ride
  • Be professional and gracious
💡 My examiner gave detailed feedback. It was clear I passed when he smiled.

BONUS TIPS FOR SUCCESS

General Tips:
  • ✓ Sleep well (most important)
  • ✓ Stay calm (easier said than done, but possible)
  • ✓ Fly the plane first (always)
  • ✓ Don't memorize (understand instead)
  • ✓ Ask for clarification (professionalism)
  • ✓ Be yourself (not fake)
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • ✗ Rushing preflight
  • ✗ Over-studying last minute
  • ✗ Staying up late night before
  • ✗ Being defensive with examiner
  • ✗ Guessing instead of asking
  • ✗ Forgetting to fly the airplane
  • ✗ Over-communicating on radio
Final Thoughts:

"The check ride doesn't test whether you can fly well on your best day. It tests whether you fly like a professional on a completely ordinary one."

Train to be the pilot the ACS describes — and the check ride will take care of itself.
