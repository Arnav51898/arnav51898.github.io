CPL Check Ride Tips:
What I Learned

After months of training and countless sim hours, I finally sat for my Commercial check ride. Here's everything I wish someone had told me — 8 real, actionable tips from my experience.

Arnav Pareek CPL + IR  ·  Delhi 8-min read

"The check ride doesn't test whether you're a perfect pilot. It tests whether you're a safe, thinking one."

The CPL check ride is one of those milestones that feels impossibly distant when you start training — and then arrives faster than you expected. When I finally walked into that briefing room, I thought I was prepared. And in many ways, I was. But the experience still taught me things that no ground school lesson or instructor could quite convey. Below, I've distilled those lessons into eight practical tips. Whether you're weeks away or deep in prep right now, I hope these save you some of the anxiety — and maybe a few mistakes — that come with doing this for the first time.

8 Tips from the Left Seat
01

Know the ACS — Not Just the Maneuvers

Most candidates study the maneuver descriptions and call it a day. Don't. The Airman Certification Standards document is the examiner's checklist. Every single task, every required element, every knowledge area they're allowed to test — it's all right there in black and white. Print it out. Annotate it. Know exactly what "satisfactory performance" looks like by their definition, not yours. When you understand the ACS, you stop guessing what the examiner wants and start flying to a known standard. That shift alone removes a huge chunk of pre-ride anxiety.

▸ Download the FAA ACS for your certificate and read the Applicant Decision-Making section twice.
02

Master the Oral Before You Touch the Plane

Here's the truth most candidates don't hear until it's too late: more check rides are failed in the briefing room than in the air. The oral examination is long, probing, and unscripted. The examiner will follow threads wherever they lead. If you stumble on weather theory, expect ten more weather questions. If you're sharp on regulations, they'll move on quickly. Prepare systems knowledge, weather, FARs, airspace, performance charts, and cross-country planning until they feel like second nature — not a memorised script, but a genuine understanding you can talk around from any angle.

▸ Do mock orals with your instructor. Have them probe your weakest areas mercilessly.
03

Verbalise Everything — Loudly and Clearly

The examiner cannot read your mind. Every scan, every decision, every deviation from the plan — narrate it. "I'm noticing a 200-foot deviation, correcting back to altitude." "Traffic at two o'clock, 1000 above, no factor." This isn't performative; it demonstrates the aeronautical decision-making that is baked into the ACS. When you think out loud, the examiner sees a safe, aware pilot. When you go quiet and just fly, they wonder what's happening inside your head — or whether anything is. The best pilots I've met talk their way through flights. Practice that habit now.

04

Brief Your Examiner Like a Captain

Before you taxi out, treat the examiner the way you'd treat a first officer on day one. Brief the flight: route, altitudes, expected weather, emergency procedures, crew coordination expectations. Ask if they have any preferences for how the flight flows. This immediately establishes CRM. It signals professional maturity. And it removes ambiguity — you won't be surprised mid-flight by something they expected you to announce. That confident pre-departure brief sets a tone that carries through the entire evaluation.

▸ "Before we depart, I'd like to brief the flight. Is that alright with you?" — just say that.
05

Fly Like It's Just Another Training Flight

This is easier said than done, but it's the most important mental shift. The moment you start flying for the examiner instead of flying the aircraft, performance degrades. Your hands get tight. Corrections become jerky. You second-guess entries you've done a hundred times. The check ride is not harder than your training flights — in fact, it's often smoother, because the pressure sharpens your focus. Trust your muscle memory. Fly the numbers you know. The examiner is there to observe, not intimidate. Treat the airplane like your aircraft and the flight like your flight.

06

Know When to Make the PIC Call

Nothing signals Pilot-in-Command maturity more than making the hard call before being asked. If weather is deteriorating on your cross-country leg, divert. Say it out loud: "I'm not comfortable continuing with those conditions. I'm diverting to an alternate." If a maneuver doesn't feel right in the setup, say "I'm going to go around and reset." Examiners aren't judging the outcome as much as the decision-making process. A pilot who recognises when something isn't right and acts accordingly is exactly what a CPL certificate is meant to produce. Indecision, or worse — pressing on when you shouldn't — is what fails check rides.

▸ SRM — Single-Pilot Resource Management. Know it. Live it. The examiner is watching for it.
07

Rest, Eat, and Show Up Sharp

This sounds basic. It isn't. Cognitive performance under pressure drops dramatically when you're sleep-deprived or hungry. I've seen candidates miss easy questions during the oral simply because they stayed up until 2 AM reviewing notes and arrived on empty. The night before your check ride: stop studying early, eat well, sleep. The knowledge is already in your head — more cramming at midnight doesn't add to it, it just adds fatigue. On check ride morning, eat a real breakfast. Arrive 20 minutes early. Sit quietly. Breathe. You've done the work. Trust it.

08

Remember: The Examiner Wants You to Pass

This might be the most underrated tip on this list. DPEs are not adversaries. They are experienced aviators who genuinely want to see you succeed. They've sat where you're sitting. They know what a nervous candidate looks like on the first maneuver before they find their groove. They will give you set-up time. They will ask leading questions if you're on the right track but not articulating it cleanly. If you make a mistake, acknowledge it calmly and correct it — that's exactly what a good pilot does. Walking in with that mindset rather than dread changes everything about how you perform.

▸ If stuck on an oral question, say: "I'd like to look that up to give you an accurate answer." That's the correct response.

Clear Skies & a Clean Logbook Entry

The CPL check ride is a rite of passage — one that demands everything you've put into training and gives back more than just a certificate. It builds a kind of quiet confidence that's hard to manufacture any other way. You learn that you can perform under real pressure, make real decisions, and hold yourself to a professional standard.

If you're heading into your check ride soon: you're ready. Trust your preparation, fly your aircraft, and enjoy the last flight before the letters after your name change. It's a remarkable feeling. I'd love to hear how it goes.
